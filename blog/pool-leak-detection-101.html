Pool Leak Detection 101: How Professionals Find Leaks

📅 February 2025⏱ 7 min read✍️ Pool Leak Inspections Team

When you call a pool leak detection company, what actually happens? Many homeowners are surprised to discover that professional leak detection is a methodical, multi-step process using specialised equipment — not just a visual look around the pool deck.

Understanding the process helps you know what you're paying for, ask the right questions, and recognise a thorough inspection from a cursory one. Here's exactly how the pros find pool leaks.

Step 1: The Initial Assessment

1

Visual inspection & homeowner interview

The technician starts by asking you about the symptoms — when did you first notice water loss? Is the pump losing prime? Are there wet spots in the yard? This history helps narrow down where to focus. A visual walk-around of the pool, equipment pad, and deck follows.

During this phase, a good technician is looking for obvious clues: efflorescence (white staining) on the shell or deck, rust staining around fittings, cracks in the plaster, loose tiles at the waterline, and the general condition of the pool equipment.

Step 2: The Bucket Test (Baseline)

2

Quantifying the water loss

Before beginning technical testing, a baseline evaporation test is often performed. A bucket of pool water is placed on a step, marked at the same level as the pool surface, and compared over time. This confirms whether meaningful water loss is occurring and at what rate.

Step 3: Pressure Line Testing

3

Isolating leaking plumbing lines

This is the most reliable method for identifying underground plumbing leaks. The technician plugs off each line individually — return lines, suction lines, main drain — and pressurises them with air or water. A line that can't hold pressure has a breach somewhere.

Pressure testing can determine not only which line is leaking, but can give a rough indication of how serious the breach is based on how quickly pressure drops. Underground leaks in return lines and suction pipes are commonly found this way — without any excavation at this stage.

Step 4: Dye Testing

4

Pinpointing exact failure points

Dye testing uses a specially formulated non-toxic dye injected near suspected leak points. When the dye is pulled toward a leak, the technician can see exactly where water is escaping. This works particularly well around fittings, pool lights, main drains, and the waterline.

Dye testing requires a trained eye and calm water — even small currents in the pool can make the results difficult to read. The technician typically uses a syringe or squeeze bottle to apply the dye precisely.

Step 5: Acoustic Leak Detection

5

Listening for underground leaks

For leaks that can't be found by pressure testing or dye alone, acoustic listening equipment is used. These sensitive ground microphones can detect the sound of water escaping underground, helping to pinpoint the location of a breach without unnecessary excavation.

This technique is particularly valuable in pools with complex underground plumbing, pools near buildings, or situations where pressure testing has confirmed a leak but the exact location is unclear.

Step 6: Underwater Structural Inspection

6

Examining the shell from inside

The technician may physically enter the pool (or use a camera) to inspect the shell, the main drain gasket, return fittings, lights, and any visible cracks in the plaster or gunite. Cracks in the floor or walls — even hairline cracks — can be significant leak sources.

Pro tip: A full 5-point inspection covers the pool structure, all plumbing lines, the light fitting, the main drain, and skimmer connections. Always ask your technician to confirm they're testing all five areas — not just one or two.

What Happens After the Leak Is Found?

Once the source is identified, the technician will explain exactly what they found, show you evidence (photos, pressure readings), and provide repair options. Common repairs include:

  • Epoxy injection — for cracks in the shell
  • Fitting replacement — for worn or cracked return jets and drain covers
  • Gasket replacement — for pool lights, skimmers, and main drains
  • Pipe repair or relining — for underground plumbing breaches
  • Deck coring & pipe replacement — for severe underground breaks

Most straightforward leaks can be repaired the same day as the inspection. More complex underground repairs may require a follow-up visit.

Why DIY Detection Has Limits

While the bucket test is useful for confirming that a leak exists, it cannot tell you where the leak is. Homeowners often spend significant time and money on guesswork — patching visible cracks, replacing gaskets — when the actual leak is underground or in a line they never considered.

Professional detection tools (pressure gauges, acoustic equipment, commercial dye kits) are simply not practical for one-off residential use. A professional inspection typically pays for itself in avoided water costs within a few months.
