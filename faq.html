How much does a pool leak inspection cost?

$195 for a pool, $225 for a spa, and $250 for a combined pool and spa inspection. Every inspection includes a written report and repair estimate. If we cannot find a leak, you owe us $0.

What does the inspection include?

Our 5-point leak analysis covers: (1) pressure testing every plumbing line, (2) hydrophone acoustic listening underwater, (3) dye tracing near suspected leaks, (4) skimmer and fitting inspection, and (5) shell and plaster check. You receive a written report plus a transparent repair estimate.

How do I know if my pool is actually leaking vs. just losing water to evaporation?

Some evaporation is normal in Southern California — up to ¼ inch per day. If you're losing more than that, you likely have a leak. The simplest DIY check is the bucket test — place a bucket of pool water on the 2nd step, match water levels, and compare the drop after 24 hours. If the pool dropped more than the bucket, you have a leak.

Is some water loss normal?

Yes. Pools naturally lose water to evaporation, splash-out, filter backwashing, and aerosolized spray from water features. ¼ inch per day is typical. More than that — especially in cooler months when evaporation is low — points to a leak.

What are the most common places pools leak?

The most frequent leak sources are: (1) cracked skimmer throats, (2) deteriorated plumbing unions under the equipment pad, (3) light niche gaskets, (4) plaster cracks in the shell, and (5) underground return or suction lines. We check every one of these on every inspection.

My water bill spiked — could it be a pool leak?

Very likely. A pinhole leak in an underground pool line can push 300–500 gallons a day into the ground, which is usually worth $100–$300 per month in water and sewer charges. It pays for itself to find it fast.

Will you inspect a drained or empty pool?

A pool needs to be filled to normal operating level and the equipment running for a proper leak inspection. If the pool is drained, we can do a visual shell inspection but cannot pressure-test or locate waterline leaks. Our guarantee (no find, no fee) applies to filled, operational pools.

How do I prepare for my inspection appointment?

Make sure the pool is filled to normal level, the equipment is running on its usual schedule, and the equipment pad and pool deck are clear and accessible. Disable any auto-fill for 24 hours before the visit so we can see the real water loss. That's it — we handle everything else.

What's the difference between pool leak detection and a general pool service company?

General pool service focuses on chemistry, cleaning, and routine maintenance. Leak detection is a specialty — it requires hydrophones, pressure test equipment, dye tracing tools, and experience diagnosing dozens of different leak failure modes. Many general pool companies refer leak problems to specialists like us.

Can you help with a pool that keeps losing prime or getting air in the pump?

Yes — that's usually a suction-side leak (between the pool and the pump). Those leaks don't always show water loss because they draw air instead of losing water when the pump runs. We use dedicated pressure and vacuum tests to find suction-side leaks on every job.

Do I need to be home during the inspection?

Not necessarily — as long as we have access to the pool area, the equipment pad, and any indoor shutoff valves we might need. Many customers leave a gate unlocked or give us the key. We'll text photos of findings as we go and email the written report when we're done.
